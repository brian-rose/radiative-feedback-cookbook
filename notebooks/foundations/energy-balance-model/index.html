Radiative Budget Analysis¶

The Energy Balance Model¶

Note

For a more comprehensive material about the energy balance model, pokearounds on the energy balance model, and introductions to simpler energy balance models like the two-box model, feel free to check out other resources like the Climate Laboratory and Chapter 2 - Global Energy Balance of the book by Hartmann (2016).

Imagine a scenario where we start off with an energy-balanced Earth, where at top of atmosphere (TOA) flux towards Earth’s surface is equal to the TOA flux going out to space.

Now if we double the concentration levels of carbon dioxide (CO2_22​) in the atmopshere - the surface temperature is going to increase through the greenhouse-effect instantaneously. However, climate processes can amplify or dampen the climate response. For example, the surface may radiate more out to space due to the surface warming, which would dampen the overall warming. On the other hand, there may be more water vapor to absorb the outgoing longwave radiation in the atmosphere due to the increased rate of evaporation from the warming, and thus amplifies the warming.

To put this into equations, let RRR be the radiative imbalance, S0S_0S0​ as the solar insolation, α\alphaα as the planetary albedo, and together, S0/4(1−α)S_0/4 (1-\alpha)S0​/4(1−α) represents the absorbed solar radiation. OLR is the outgoing longwave radiation. At equilibrium where the incoming flux equals to the outgoing flux, R=0R = 0R=0.

R=Fin−FoutR = F_{in} - F_{out}R=Fin​−Fout​
R=S0/4(1−α)−OLRR = S_0/4 (1-\alpha) - OLRR=S0​/4(1−α)−OLR

In this case, the addition of CO2_{2}2​ induces a change in the radiative flux at the top of the atmosphere, and is quantified as the raditive forcing F. The climate responds to the radiative imbalance by changing the global mean temperature. We can relate the global mean surface temperature to the external perturbation and the radiative imbalance at TOA by the following equation:

R=F+λTR = F + \lambda TR=F+λT

where RRR is the net TOA flux anomaly (positive downward), FFF is the effective radiative forcing, λ\lambdaλ is the feedback parameter, also known as the climate response parameter, and TTT is the global mean surface temperature anomaly, relative to the temperature at equilibrium.

Other typical conventions of the same values used within the community:

  • R=NR = NR=N (e.g., Gregory et al. (2004));
  • R=HR = HR=H (e.g., Dessler & Zelinka (2015));
  • F=ΔQF = \Delta QF=ΔQ (e.g., Bony et al. (2006));
  • λ=α\lambda = \alphaλ=α (e.g., Gregory et al. (2004));
Note

As you explore the feedback-forcing space, you may stumble upon the term climate sensitivity, which is the relationship between the magnitude of the climate change response and the doubling-CO2_22​ forcing with the unit in Kelvin.

ECS=1λECS = \frac{1}{\lambda}ECS=λ1​

This metric tells how much the climate system would warm per unit of radiative forcing (typically doubling of CO2_{2}2​). Ch.10 Climate Sensitivity and Feedback Mechanisms of the book by Hartmann (2016) and the chapter Climate Feedbacks in the Encyclopedia of Atmospheric Sciences Dessler & Zelinka, 2015 provide a holistic overview on the topic, and Sherwood et al. (2020) would provide the most up-to-date understanding on equilibrium climate sensitivity (ECS) within the community.

Radiative Feedback (λ)(\lambda)(λ)¶

So, what is a feedback and why does it matter?¶

Feedback is the climate system response to an external radiative forcing Hansen et al., 1984, Bony et al., 2006. When the global mean surface temperature changes, climate variables may change as well. These climate variable changes would influence the Earth’s radiation balance and contribute to the radiative response.

Note that feedback is different from the radiative response, where the former has the unit of W m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1 and is quantified by the feedback parameter λ\lambdaλ, while the latter has the unit of W m−2^{-2}−2, quantified by λT\lambda TλT.

Let xxx be a vector representing an ensemble of climate variables like atmospheric temperature, water vapor, surface ice and snow. The formal definition of the system’s total feedback parameter, which is the strength of the climate system’s net feedback, is as follow bony:2006a:

In the following subsections, we will discuss each feedback mechanism in detail and how the mechanism would feed back into enhancing or muting the response.

Types of Feedbacks¶

Feedbacks below are listed following Sherwood et al., 2020 and are limited to feedbacks that directly affect the top-of-the-atmosphere (TOA) radiation budget, and respond to surface temperature mostly through physical processes Bony et al., 2006:

  1. Planck Feedback
  2. Surface Albedo Feedback
  3. Water Vapor Feedback
  4. Lapse Rate Feedback
  5. Cloud Feedback

The list above is not exhaustive. Feedbacks like the carbon cycle feedback are not included due to the carbon dioxide level being prescribed for the experimental set-up in Sherwood et al. (2020). Other feedbacks like the atmospheric ozone feedback and stratospheric feedback are not included as they are not normally quantified Sherwood et al., 2020.

1. Planck “Feedback” (Negative under warming)¶

Planck feedback is the extra longwave (LW) emission to space from a vertically uniform warming of the surface and atmosphere, while holding all climate variable unchanged.

The Planck feedback can be explained by the Stefan-Boltzmann law, which states that the total energy radiated per unit surface area per unit time is directly proportional to the fourth power of the black body’s temperature.

F=σT4F = \sigma T^4F=σT4
(1)

where σ\sigmaσ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67×10−85.67 \times 10^{-8}5.67×10−8 W m−2^{-2}−2 K−4^{-4}−4). If everything else is held constant, we can calculate the rate of change of TOA flux due to the change in surface warming by taking the derivative of the Stefan-Boltzmann law. Taking the Earth’s average outgoing longwave radiation as 240 W m−2^{−2}−2 (e.g., Loeb et al. (2018)) for the global effective emission temperature TeT_{e}Te​ as 255 K, and substitute it as follow:

−4σTe3≈−3.76 W m−2 K−1-4\sigma T_e^3 \approx - 3.76~\text{W}~\text{m}^{-2}~\text{K}^{-1}−4σTe3​≈−3.76 W m−2 K−1
(2)

If we include the planetary emissivity ϵ\epsilonϵ, we will get λPlanck≈−3.3\lambda_{Planck} \approx -3.3λPlanck​≈−3.3 W m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1, which is close to observations Dessler, 2013 and global climate models Caldwell et al., 2016).

Simply put, the more you heat, the more LW radiation go out.

2. Surface Albedo Feedback (Positive under warming)¶

The surface albedo feedback, also known as the ice-albedo feedback, pertains primarily to the high latitudes, where snow and ice are present. Snow and ice have very high albedo (reflectivity). Snow has the highest albedo (up to 90%), while sea ice albedo ranges from 50-70%. Melt ponds further reduce the albedo, and as ice recedes, open ocean can be as low as 6%.

This process consitutes a positive feedback loop: melt of snow and ice decreases albedo, causing additional radiation absorption. More absorption causes temperatures to rise, which further contributes to melt, restarting the cycle.

5. Cloud Feedback¶

The cloud feedback mechanism is the most complex feedback discussed in this tutorial. There are several reasons for this. Clouds interact with both shortwave (SW) solar radiation and longwave (LW) terrestrial radiation and exert varying radiative effects depending on their properties (cloud type, height, optical depth, liquid and ice water content, particule sizes, etc).

Generally, high-level clouds do not interact much with solar radiation but easily absorb terrestrial radiation, preventing its loss to space and contribute to warming. Low-level clouds reflect solar radiation during the day, which contributes to cooling, but absorb some terrestrial radiation, contributing to warming (particularly at night!). Thus, high-level clouds tend to exert a positive climate feedback, while low-level clouds tend to exert an overall negative climate feedback.


Credit: Paulo Ceppi

Cloud feedbacks produced by climate models tend to vary greatly, which is largely attributed to the issue of scale in modern GCMs (ESMs??). GCMs are at resolutions much too large to resolve the microscopic processes dictating the formation and properties of clouds. Cloud formation and lifetime is additionally complex as it is highly dependent on atmospheric aerosol loading, which is another complex discussion. Of course, clouds are also intrinsically tied to atmospheric water vapor content.
